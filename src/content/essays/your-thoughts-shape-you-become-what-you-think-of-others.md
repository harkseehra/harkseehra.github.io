> I observe the same in people I encounter. My question today for whoever reads this is: **How do you think one becomes paranoid and seemingly irrational without understanding the emotions and real behavior of others?**

Conclusions are often drawn based on two factors: personal feelings or what has been learned from the beginning of one’s existence. Since the moment of birth, individuals pick up on the behaviors of others, and it is fortunate if those people are heroes, shaping a worldview rooted in positivity.

Our thinking is molded by the negative things we witness throughout our lives. The more negative experiences we encounter, the more we internalize fear. Unfortunately, social media, governed by self-proclaimed intellectuals, amplifies this fear by presenting a constant stream of alarming content.

People absorb new fears daily, as videos circulate depicting various events, and individuals freely express their opinions in lengthy comments. The impact is felt personally, affecting mental health and leading to long-term misconceptions about the practical world.

> ####  As spectators, individuals absorb information without being certain of its accuracy, yet they integrate it into their belief systems based on personal feelings and biases.

Expressing feelings has become increasingly simplified, with individuals categorizing themselves as liberal, conservative, or adherents of specific ideologies without explaining the nuances of their emotions. The use of labels substitutes for genuine self-feeling, reducing complex emotions to a single word that may not authentically represent one’s feelings.

Consider a scenario where you observe a stranger on a bus and conclude that they seem depressed and anxious. Unconsciously, facial expressions and behavior may contribute to this judgment, but consciously, the ability to sense and interpret such emotions comes from personal experiences with similar feelings.

Depression and anxiety are sensed as shared vibes between individuals who have suffered to a certain degree, supported scientifically by the effects of oxygen and sound. However, the joyful person, unfamiliar with these emotions, may perceive the behaviors as strange unless they understand the underlying issues.

Social media claims to be supportive of modern-day issues like depression and anxiety, but this support is often commercialized. The trade of pseudo-intellectuals aims not to improve the world but to make everything marketable, turning individuals into products pursued by society, undermining personal needs and true selves.

This commodification diminishes creativity and individuality, creating a paradox where the world seemingly supports depression and anxiety but fails to address them effectively. Services claim to be the last savior, encouraging people to wait until they reach a critical stage before seeking help.

> #### The comfort created for depressed individuals contradicts the nature of these conditions, which should ideally be addressed through rehabilitation.

Reflecting on your thinking, which aligns 100% with my own, the truth on the page is that we are becoming what we think. The headline captures the essence that what one thinks of others is a reflection of their own state. 

The call to action is to be a vigilant spectator, fully understand your feelings, engage in self-reflection, and avoid adopting new thoughts without careful consideration. 

> #### This approach minimizes conflicts and enhances thought clarity, ensuring a more authentic understanding of oneself and others.
